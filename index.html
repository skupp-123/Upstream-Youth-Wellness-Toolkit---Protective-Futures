Upstream Youth Wellness Framework: Protective Futures

A peer-powered relational framework for youth thriving. Ages 14 to 24. North-Central Vancouver Island, BC.

The Framework

A Braided Approach to Youth Thriving

This framework is built on the structure of a braid with multiple strands woven in order to become stronger together. It is not a program with a waitlist, but rather upstream infrastructure to build on resilience, and make crisis less likely.[1]

The framework follows youth direction and is designed to be adapted to each community's specific needs, protocols, and knowledge systems with local Nation direction brought in at the beginning.[2]

Equity-deserving youth (those facing the greatest structural barriers) are given lead voices at the table. This includes Indigenous youth, PWLLE (People with Lived and Living Experience), 2SLGBTQ+, neurodivergent youth, youth experiencing poverty or housing instability, racialized youth, and youth in or aged-out from government care. BC and Canadian public health research is unambiguous: these groups face compounding upstream disadvantage, therefore this is where upstream investment is the highest priority.[4]

🌱
Outer Braid: Belonging and Connection
Connection to nature · safe housing · food security · trusted relationships · school and community connectedness · the protective buffer that makes every other stream possible[1]
🔥
Middle Braid: Identity and Cultural Grounding
Cultural teachings · language · ceremony · land connection · Indigenous identity as protective factor · Harm reduction as education, empowerment, ceremony, and a way to decolonize care[3] · decolonization of body, mind, and spirit
⭐
Inner Braid: Purpose, Agency, and Economic Dignity
Paid youth roles ($25/hr)[5] · credentials and employment pathways · choice and agency · meaningful contribution · a relationship with the future as a protective factor[9]
🤝
Cross-Braid: Trusted Adults and Mentorship
The strand that weaves through everything. Youth consistently name trusted adults as their most reliable source of support[9] · mentors · Elders · PWLLE facilitators · cultural knowledge carriers — present in every stream, cannot be separated
At a Glance
Who It's For
  • Youth ages 14–24 in North Island communities[7]
  • Emphasis on Indigenous youth, LGBTQ2S+ youth, and youth experiencing disconnection[4]
  • Youth who are not yet in crisis. Upstream, not downstream[1]
  • Youth in Comox Valley, Campbell River, and surrounding territories
Potential Leadership Principles
  • If Indigenous youth want to take a lead in co-developing, then suggest Indigenous-led governance, with Nations shaping direction from the start (K'ómoks First Nation, We Wai Kai Nation, Wei Wai Kum Nation)
  • Elders, Matriarchs, and Knowledge Keepers could help youth shape cultural content
  • Youth with Living and Lived Experience of substance use and/or barriers to accessing services or supports to wellness could co-create and co-deliver
  • Youth would be paid decision-makers ($25/hr honoraria as per BCCDC Peer Payment Standards)
Potential Braided Funding Model
  • No single funder owns the work
  • First Nations Health Authority (FNHA)
  • Public Health Agency of Canada · YSUPP
  • Vancouver Foundation · McConnell Foundation
  • Indigenous Services Canada · BC Ministry of Health
  • Pilot range: $250K to $750K

A note on roles: This framework would not be owned by any single organization or individual, but instead follow youth direction. Supporting partners, including Nations, community organizations, health systems navigators and connectors, would play a role in bridging systems and removing barriers; not in leading or directing youth decisions. This framework belongs to the youth and communities it serves.

Program Architecture

Six Program Streams — Choose Your Own Adventure

Youth choose which streams they want to engage with, how deep they go, and what combination makes sense for them. No single pathway is required. Each stream connects to the others, and every entry point leads somewhere.

STREAM 1
Belonging & Connection
The entry point for everything else. Safe, welcoming spaces · peer relationships · school and community connection · low-barrier, food-first gathering · no intake forms, no waitlist.[1] This is the stream that makes all others possible.

Local examples: Foundry Comox Valley · Foundry Campbell River · Wachiay Friendship Centre
STREAM 2
Identity & Cultural Grounding
Indigenous identity as protective factor · Elder-led teachings · land-based learning · Nation-specific protocols · canoe teachings · cedar weaving · breath and embodiment practices.[2,3]

Local examples: N'alag̱a (Avis O'Brien) Hase' Breath of Life — Kwakwaka'wakw land-based practices addressing overdose and harm reduction as an act of resistance · Nuu-chah-nulth Youth Warrior Family — land-based Indigenous youth leadership, West Vancouver Island · Nala'atsi Aboriginal Education SD71 · Comox Lake Park Society Watershed Canoe Tour — 29' voyageur-style canoe, adaptive and inclusive equipment available
STREAM 3
Meaningful Activity — Somewhere to Be
Music · mural art · digital storytelling · film · podcasts · hip hop nights · land-based activity · sport · cooking · gaming. The specific activity matters less than showing up somewhere with other people.[1]

Local examples: Comox Valley Art Gallery Youth Media Project — digital storytelling and media arts · Unbroken Chain — youth hip hop nights, harm reduction, and mentoring · Foundry Comox Valley youth programming
STREAM 4
Economic Dignity & Future Self
A changed relationship with the future is itself a protective factor.[9] Paid youth roles · credentials · employment pathways · skills that open doors.

Local examples: North Island College — HCAP Mental Health and Addictions Worker program (paid while you train) · WorkBC Employment Services for Youth · ISET Indigenous Skills and Employment Training · Not Just Naloxone (FNHA) train-the-trainer · First Aid certification
STREAM 5
Harm Reduction as Literacy
Not a separate service youth opt into — basic health literacy woven through all streams. Overdose recognition and response · naloxone · drug checking · knowing your rights · FNHA "With Open Arms" principles[3] · paid school presenter roles for youth.[9]

Local examples: VCH Street Degree in Overdose Prevention — free online certification · Toward the Heart — naloxone training and take-home kits, BC-wide · AVI Health North Island — CRCL harm reduction program · Hase' Breath of Life — harm reduction as cultural resistance
STREAM 6
Trusted Adults & Mentorship
The stream most upstream prevention frameworks are missing. Recruiting, training, and paying mentors and PWLLE facilitators to be consistently present across all other streams.[9] Monthly Youth Table · advocacy training · potential youth-led research · youth are decision-makers, not consultees.[11]

Local examples: Nuu-chah-nulth Youth Warrior Family — in-community mentors with renewed purpose · Foundry Comox Valley (JHS) youth worker relationships · Unbroken Chain mentoring · K'ómoks First Nation Youth Coordinator and Harm Reduction Coordinator · BCCSU Youth Advisory Council model

Participatory Design · Genuine Authority

Youth Co-Design Workshop

Youth have a voice to co-design and build sections they want from the 6-stream framework so that lived experience shapes what gets made. Then they share ideas about how each stream can be recognized: dual-credits for high school and college? Certificates for courses to add to their resume? Micro-credentials for streams to count towards post-secondary education? Cultural and land-based activities to count towards high-school completion credits? Listening sessions should have food, fun and be low-barrier to show up to.

01
You Know Your Stuff
Your lived experience is your expertise! Young people know their communities, their needs, and what has and hasn't worked way better than anyone parachuting in from outside.
02
You Get Paid
Youth who contribute to design and decision-making are compensated at $25/hr. Your time and knowledge have value.
03
Culture Comes First
Nothing moves forward without Indigenous cultural grounding and Elder or Knowledge Keeper guidance.
Workshop Structure

A Half-Day Session

1
45 MIN · ARRIVAL & GROUNDING
Show Up, Eat, Get Comfortable
Opening by an Elder or Knowledge Keeper from the host Nation. Land acknowledgment. Food, coffee, drinks. People arrive at their own pace. No launching into agenda until the room feels settled and people know each other a little.
Elder Welcome Real Food Chill Icebreaker Ground Rules Together
2
60 MIN · LISTENING
What Actually Works + What Doesn't from Youth Perspective
Small group conversations co-led with PWLLE facilitators — people who've been in similar spots. Prompts around youth wellness, what makes it hard to ask for help, what "feeling safe" actually means, and what programs have gotten badly wrong. What comes out of the room stays in the room. Youth own it.
Small Groups Visual Mapping Anonymous Input Option Co-led with PWLLE
3
60 MIN · BUILDING
Design What You'd Actually Want to Show Up To
Youth design activities, spaces, and support structures — not boxed in by what's currently fundable or what sounds good in a grant proposal. Drawing, mapping, role-playing, storytelling — whatever works. Everything gets taken seriously, written down, and actually fed back into how this thing gets built.
Prototype Activities Draw It Out Arts-Based Options Real Documentation
4
30 MIN · CLOSING
What We Heard, What Happens Next, See You Around
Make clear who's following up, when, and see if any youth want to help lead next steps. Honoraria go out before anyone leaves. Closed with cultural protocol if appropriate. Opportunity for youth to continue supports at John Howard/Foundry as needed.

Stories of Impact · Composite Portraits

Three Youth.
Three Paths.
One Framework.

These are composite portraits drawn from patterns in the research, not from a program that exists yet. They show what an upstream youth wellness framework could look like when it's working, leading with connection.

they / them
Jordan
17 · Comox Valley
"I didn't need someone to fix me. I needed somewhere I didn't have to explain myself."
Where They Started
Jordan is a trans youth who had stopped going to most school programs — not because of disengagement, but because every space required them to justify their existence before anything else could happen. Home was complicated. The usual youth drop-ins felt like they'd been designed for someone else.
What Connected
Stream 3 (Meaningful Activity) opened the door — a music production workshop with no rules about who you had to be to walk in. Jordan started showing up. Then kept showing up. The PWLLE facilitator didn't make a big deal of it. Eventually, Jordan joined the Youth Advisory Circle.
Program Streams
3: Meaningful Activity 6: Trusted Adults 1: Belonging
Shift
Jordan now facilitates a peer support circle for other 2SLGBTQ+ youth — paid, confident, and designing the space they once needed. The system didn't fix Jordan. Jordan improved the system.
she / her
Maya
19 · Campbell River · We Wai Kum Nation
"I knew the land. I just didn't know that knowing the land was something worth something."
Where She Started
Maya, from We Wai Kum Nation, had been hearing her whole life that success meant leaving. Programs she'd encountered rarely saw her Nation's knowledge and language as assets — they were backdrop, not foundation. She wasn't in crisis. She was just unseen.
What Connected
Stream 2 (Identity and Cultural Grounding) was co-led by Elders from her Nation — not facilitated by an outside organization using cultural content as curriculum decoration. For the first time, Maya's knowledge was the knowledge being centered. She began a peer facilitation certification.
Program Streams
2: Identity & Culture 4: Economic Dignity 5: Harm Reduction Literacy
Shift
Maya completed a peer facilitation certification and is now co-delivering harm reduction education in Campbell River schools — carrying knowledge from her Nation into rooms that have never heard it before. She didn't leave. She built something worth staying for.
he / him
Ezra
21 · Comox Valley
"Every program I'd been in felt like it was waiting for me to fail so they could help me."
Where He Started
Ezra had experience with substances, some involvement with the justice system, and a long history of being the kind of youth that systems are designed to manage rather than believe in. He'd aged out of most youth services. He was working part-time and staying afloat — but only just.
What Connected
A PWLLE outreach worker — someone who'd been in a similar place — connected Ezra to Stream F and the paid Youth Advisory role. No intake form asking him to rehearse his worst moments. Just: "We're building something. Are you in?" He was in.
Program Streams
4: Economic Dignity 6: Trusted Adults 5: Harm Reduction Literacy
Shift
Ezra now holds a peer support certification and is a Youth Governance Circle member. He trains other young people in overdose response. He didn't become a success story because someone saved him — he became one because he was given actual authority, actual pay, and actual trust.
Jordan, Maya, and Ezra don't share a diagnosis.
They share a need for belonging, purpose, and community.

An upstream youth wellness framework is the infrastructure that makes that possible — not one program, not one person, but a braided set of relationships, paid roles, cultural grounding, and genuine youth authority. When youth are connected to land, culture, and their own gifts, communities feel the difference.

Systems Thinking · Root Causes

What's Driving the Toxic Drug Poisoning Crisis

The visible crisis — overdoses, emergency calls, youth disengagement — sits at the tip of a much larger iceberg. Most systems respond only to what they can see. This framework works below the waterline.

Where most systems respond

At the Events layer — emergency care, crisis lines, detox services. Vital and necessary. But responding to events alone, without addressing patterns and structures, means the same events keep recurring.

Where this framework works

At the Structures and Mental Models layers — connecting fragmented coordination, advocating for school credit recognition of cultural programming, and reframing whose knowledge counts as evidence. Slower, but it changes the conditions that produce the events.

The deepest work

Mental models shift slowest and last longest. When "culture is the intervention" becomes a shared belief held by one school board, one funder, one Nation — the shape of the whole system changes downstream.

Indigenous Healing · Cultural Empowerment · [14]

N'alag̱a (Avis O'Brien). (2021–present). Hase' Breath of Life. N'alag̱a Consulting. Haida and Kwakwaka'wakw land-based cultural empowerment facilitator. 12-week curriculum using Kwakwaka'wakw ancestral knowledge, breath, and embodiment practices as self-regulation tools. Addresses drug poisoning, harm reduction as resistance to the opioid epidemic, and overdose loss. Developed for facilitators, Elders, and mental health professionals. Also includes cedar weaving, drumming, singing, and ceremony as decolonization and healing practices. Workshop series also includes suicide prevention and relapse prevention through an Indigenous lens. Directly aligns with Streams 2 and 5 of this framework.
